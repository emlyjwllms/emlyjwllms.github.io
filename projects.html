FV Eulerian Solver for 1D Nonlinear Simplified Navier-Stokes Equations

AE 410: Computational Aerodynamics, UIUC (2021)

Euler equations can be obtained from the nonlinear, 1D Navier-Stokes equations assuming vanishing viscosity and thermal conductivity: \begin{align} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \rho u}{\partial x} &= 0 \\ \frac{\partial \rho u}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x} (\rho u^2 + p) = \frac{\partial \tau}{\partial x} &= 0 \\ \frac{\partial \rho E}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[(\rho E + p)u] = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(\tau u - q) &= 0 \end{align} where $\rho$ is the fluid density, $u$ is the fluid velocity, and $p$ is the thermodynamic pressure. Total energy density is defined as: \begin{align} \rho E = \rho e + \frac{1}{2} \rho u^2 \end{align} The pressure is related to $\rho E$ by the equation of state: \begin{align} p = (\gamma-1)\bigg(\rho E - \frac{1}{2} \rho u^2 \bigg) \end{align} The Euler equations were solved using Roe's method to discretize the convective fluxes using a finite-volume method with equally spaced volumes to span the flow volume from 0 to 1. The characteristic boundary condition was used for the subsonic outlet and numerical flux functions were computed using the Roe scheme. Post-shock initial conditions were found using the Rankine-Hugoniot equations. Mass flow surrounding the stationary shock occuring at $x = 0.5$ is shown below, with a number of waves being shed by the shock and traveling downstream as expected.

FD Solver for 1D Linear Euler Equations

AE 410: Computational Aerodynamics, UIUC (2021)

To derive the 1D linear acoustic equations in $p$ and $\rho$, we start from the linearized Euler equations, neglecting the energy equation: \begin{align} \frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + K_0 \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} &= 0 \\ \bar{\rho} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial p}{\partial x} &= 0 \end{align} where $K_0(x) = \bar{\rho}(x)\bar{p}(x)$ is the bulk modulus of compressibility of the gas, where $\bar{\rho}(x)$ and $\bar{p}(x)$ are the flow background density and pressure, and $p(x,t)$ and $u(x,t)$ are the unknowns of the problem. We consider a fluid in a channel of unit width and assume that the background velocity of the flow is negligible ($\bar{u}=0$). We wish to study the equations of linear acoustics in a layered medium in which $\bar{\rho}$ and $K_0$ are piecewise constant functions of $x$. We define the speed of sound $c(x)$ and the impedance $Z(x)$ as: \begin{align} c(x) &= \sqrt{\frac{K_0(x)}{\bar{\rho}(x)}} \\ Z(x) &= \bar{\rho}(x)c(x) \end{align} A finite-difference solver was developed to solve the linear acoustic equations on a finite domain $x \in [-5,5]$. The boundary at $x=5$ models a solid wall ($u(5,t)=0$), while at $x=-5$, a non-reflecting boundary condition is imposed such that the amplitude of the incoming characteristic is zero. The initial conditions were given as exponential functions. The equations were integrated for at least 10 seconds using 151 points in the $x$-direction. The system of equations was rearranged in the following form: \begin{align} \frac{\partial q}{\partial t} + A\frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = 0 \end{align} where $q = [p,u]^T$ and $A$ = $[$ $0$ $K_0$ ; $1/\bar{\rho}$ $0$ $]$ = $[$ $0$ $cZ$ ; $c/Z$ $0$ $]$. Eigenvalues of A were determined to represent the speed of sound $c(x)$. Using the characteristic variables, boundary conditions that needed to be applied at $x=5$ to achieve a solid wall and $x=-5$ to achieve a non-reflection condition were determined by defining $w = X^{-1}q$ and $\Lambda = X^{-1}AX$: \begin{align} \frac{\partial w}{\partial t} + \Lambda\frac{\partial w}{\partial x} &= 0 \end{align} The specular reflection at the right boundary is defined as $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x=5) = \frac{\partial w_2}{\partial t}(x=5) = 0$. The non-reflection at the left boundary is enforced by prescribing $\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}(x=-5) = 0$. Three FD schemes were implemented: 2nd-order central, 3rd-order biased, and 6th-order Padé. The equations were integrated and then pressure and velocity profiles at different times are captured.

2D Rotational Flow

ATMS 502/CSE 566: Numerical Fluid Dynamics, UIUC (2021)

For this project, 2D linear advection is solved via fractional (directional) step splitting with a circular field initial conditions and 0-gradient boundary conditions. The rotational flow field is constant with time and integrated using 3 different methods: Lax-Wendroff, Takacs, and Crowley. The computational domain is a 2D staggered C-grid. All methods are 2-time-level and 1D. Directional splitting applies advection in the $x$-direction followed by the $y$-direction. Up to 3 ghost points are required (for Crowley 6th order).

1D Advective Flow

ATMS 502/CSE 566: Numerical Fluid Dynamics, UIUC (2021)

For this project, we needed to solve the 1D advection (transport) equation for a variable $q$ in linear and nonlinear cases: \begin{align} \text{Linear: } \frac{\partial q}{\partial t} &= -c \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} \\ \text{Nonlinear: } \frac{\partial q}{\partial t} &= -q \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} \end{align} The solution domain is a 1D mesh (grid) of $nx$ points. The PDEs above are solved for each point on this mesh. The initial condition is a single sine wave with a wavelength of $nx\Delta x$ with a periodic boundary condition (i.e. the wave "exits" on the right side of the domain and "re-enters" on the left side for speed $c > 0$). The Lax-Wendroff scheme with forward time differencing and centered space differencing is employed to solve these PDEs: \begin{align} q_j^{n+1} &= q_j^n - \frac{\nu}{2}(q_{j+1}^n - q_{j-1}^n) \\ &+ \sigma(q_{j+1}^n - 2q_j^n + q_{j-1}^n) \end{align} where $q$ is the scalar field that is being advected, $n$ is the time level, $j$ is indexing the $nx$ grid points, $\nu$ is the Courant number, and $\sigma = \nu^2/2$. For the linear case, $\nu = c\Delta t/\Delta x$, and $q_j^n \Delta t/\Delta x$ otherwise. Both linear and nonlinear cases were simulated with a timestep $\Delta t = 0.05$ for 150 steps. The simulations show the formation of a shock with nonlinear advection, while the linear case results in a stabilizing solution.

Compressible Boundary Layer over Flat Plate

AE 498: Hypersonic Aerothermodynamics, UIUC (2020)

For this project, we integrated a compressible boundary layer over a flat plate with zero pressure gradient, under the assumption of calorically perfect gas. The governing equations are: \begin{align} & (Cf'')' + ff' = 0 \\ & (Cg') + \text{Pr} fg' + \text{Pr} C(\gamma - 1) \text{M}_e^2f''^2 = 0 \end{align}

where $f'(\eta) = u/V_e$ and $g(\eta) = T/T_e$. These equations are subject to the following boundary conditions:

\begin{align} & \text{Wall: } f(0) = f'(0) = 0 \text{, } g(0) = g_w \text{ or } g'(0) = 0 \\ & \text{Edge: } f'(\infty) = g(\infty) = 1 \end{align} The solution procedure is to use a shooting technique. The state vector of unknows is $F = (f,f',f'',g,g')$. As input parameters, we need to define $\text{M}_e, \text{Pr}, \gamma$ and an expression for the Chapman-Rubesin parameter $C = (\rho\mu)/(\rho_e \mu_e)$. Using a power law of exponent $\sigma$ for the viscosity $\mu/\mu_e = (T/T_e)^\sigma$. Using the ideal gas law, we have $C = (\rho\mu)/(\rho_e \mu_e) = g^{\sigma - 1}$. The governing equations can be rewritten: \begin{align} f''' &= -\frac{f''}{g^{\sigma-1}}[(\sigma-1)g^{\sigma-2}g' + f] \\ g'' &= -\frac{1}{g^{\sigma-1}}[(\sigma-1)g^{\sigma-2}g'^2 + \text{Pr}fg' \\ & + \text{Pr}g^{\sigma-1}(\gamma-1)\text{M}_e^2f''^2] = 0 \end{align} We assumed $\sigma = 0.75, \text{Pr} = 0.72, \text{ and } \gamma = 1.4$. We considered a boundary layer with the following edge conditions: $\text{M}_e = 5$, $\text{Re}_{e,x} = 10,000$, $T_e = 223 \text{ K}$, $P_e = 1000 \text{ Pa}$, $\mu_e = 1.2 \times 10^{-5}$ Pa-s. Self-similar boundary layer temperature and velocity profiles for adiabatic and isothermal walls with $T_w/T_E = 6$ were plotted using Python. We also plotted dimensional boundary layer profiles of static (dashed) temperature, stagnation (solid) temperature, and streamwise velocity for the adiabatic wall, isothermal wall with $T_w/T_e = 4$, and isothermal wall with $T_w/T_e = 8$.
Skin friction coefficients $C_f$ and Stanton numbers $\text{ St }$ were computed for the three cases analyzed: \begin{align} & C_f = \sqrt{2}C_w\frac{f''(0)}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_{e,x}}} \\ & \text{St } = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\frac{C_w}{\text{Pr}}\frac{T_e}{T_{aw}-T_w}\frac{g'(0)}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_{e,x}}} \end{align} where $C_w = g_w^{\sigma-1}$ and $T_{aw} = T_e + \sqrt{\text{Pr}}(T_{0e}-T_e)$. These calculated values are in agreement with the work of Van Driest.

Hypersonic Limit of Inviscid Flow

AE 498: Hypersonic Aerothermodynamics, UIUC (2020)

While viscous effects are the essence of hypersonic flows, it is convenient to analyze the limit where the flow in inviscid. In this limit, transport phenomena such as viscosity, thermal conduction, diffusion, and high temperature effects are neglected. The limit for inviscid flow is satisfied when the Reynolds number is moderately large. This is preferentially obtained in freestream regions away from surfaces. From the compressible flow theory, there are two fundamental angles that describe the behavior of a streamline across the shock: streamline deflection angle $\theta$ and the angle of incidence (wave angle) $\beta$. The integral form of conservation equations across an oblique shock take the form: \begin{align} \rho_1 V_{1n} &= \rho_2 V_{2n} \\ \rho_1 V^2_{1n} + p_1 &= \rho_2 V^2_{2n} + p_2 \\ V_{1t} &= V_{2t} \\ h_{0_{1}} = h_1 + \frac{V^2_{1n}}{2} &= h_2 + \frac{V^2_{2n}}{2} = h_{0_{2}} \end{align} These equations lead to the Rankine-Hugoniot jump conditions: \begin{align} \frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1} &= \frac{(\gamma+1)M^2_{1n}}{2+(\gamma-1)M^2_{1n}} \\ \frac{p_2}{p_1} &= 1 + \frac{2\gamma}{\gamma+1}(M^2_{1n}-1) \\ \frac{T_2}{T_1} &= \frac{p_2}{p_1} \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2} \\ \frac{u_{2n}}{u_{1n}} &= \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2} \\ M^2_{2n} &= \frac{2+(\gamma-1)M^2_{1n}}{2\gamma M^2_{1n}+1-\gamma} \end{align} $M_{1n}$ refers to the normal Mach number such that $M_{1n} = u_{1n}/a_1$ where $u_{1n} = u_1 \sin{\beta}$ is the flow velocity normal to the shock and $u_{1t} = u_1 \cos{\beta}$ is the tangential flow velocity. The corresponding components of the post-shock gas are then derived as $u_{2n} = u_2 \sin{(\beta-\theta)}$ and $u_{2t} = u_2 \cos{(\beta-\theta)}$ with $M_{1n} = M_1 \sin{\beta}$ and $M_{2n} = M_2 \sin{(\beta-\theta)}$ for the Mach numbers in the normal direction (the former defined as Tsien's hypersonics similarity parameter). A relation between the wave angle $\beta$ and the angle of streamline deflection $\theta$ can be obtained using the ratio $u_{2n}/u_{1n} = \tan{(\beta-\theta)}/\tan{\beta}$ along with the Rankine-Hugoniot conditions. The $\theta-\beta-M$ relation is then derived and shown in the plot below next to the Rankine-Hugoniot ratios as the normal incoming Mach number approaches infinity: \begin{align} \tan{\theta} = \frac{2\cot{\beta}(M^2_1 \sin^2{\beta} - 1)}{2 + M^2_1 [\gamma + \cos{(2\beta)}]} \end{align} In the hypersonic limit, the pressure and temperature ratios increase unboundedly with the squared Mach number. The density reaches a maximum compression ratio of $(\gamma + 1)/(\gamma - 1) \approx 6$. The proportionality of the temperature ratio with the squared Mach number at large Mach numbers is the prediction of unrealistically high temperatures in the shock layer. The expansion of a supersonic flow can be achieved through a convex corner, which generates an expansion fan through which the flow is accelerated and expanded isentropically. In contrast to shock waves, the expansion fan occupies a finite-thickness region of space. Analysis of the stream deflection is achieved though the Prandtl-Meyer function.
