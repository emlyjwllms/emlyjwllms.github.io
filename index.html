Research

Numerics and Unsteady Flows Group [NUF]
Advisor: Andres Goza, PhD [agoza@illinois.edu]

I'm currently exploring how to make the immersed boundary (IB) method higher order as it is applied to viscous, incompressible Navier Stokes equations, which are spatially discretized using the method of lines. I'm employing the immersed boundary smooth extension method to accomplish this goal.

The immersed boundary smooth extension (IBSE) method poses a higher-order partial differential equation outside of the physical domain, with boundary conditions matching the exact solution, leading to high-order accuracy. The first figure below highlights how, for generic problems, the IB method produces discretizations that are only first-order accurate in the vicinity of domain boundaries or fluid-structure interfaces. However, the process of making the domain boundary forcing function result in a continuous function across the entire domain, rather than a piecewise, allows the function to be increasingly differentiable, making the solution able to achieve higher-order accuracy overall.

The above figures show the extended forcing made to result in a continuous derivative solution at the boundaries of the physical domain from Stein, Guy, and Thomases (2017).

These papers offer more insight into the problem that I'm investigating:

  • Qadeer, S., and Griffith, B. The Smooth Forcing Extension Method: A High-Order Technique for Solving Elliptic Equations on Complex Domains. March 2020, arXiv:2003.04924v1.
  • Stein, D., Guy, R., and Thomases, B. Immersed Boundary Smooth Extension (IBSE): A high-order method for solving incompressible flows in arbitrary smooth domains. Journal of Computational Physics, Volume 335, April 2017, Pages 155-178, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcp.2017.01.010.
  • Colonius, T., and Taira, K. A fast immersed boundary method using a nullspace approach and multi-domain far-field boundary conditions. Computer Methods in Applied Mechanics and Engineering, Volume 197, Issues 25-28, April 2008, Pages 2131-2146, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cma.2007.08.014.

For Fall 2020, I'm currently using TensorFlow neural networks to fit 3D potential energy surfaces to acquire the reaction rates of the hydrogen cyanide (HCN) system. Optimization and regularization are being used on permutationally invariant polynomials with uncertainty quantification to determine the accuracy needed for the diatomic potential fit to be acceptable.

Machine learning techniques are very powerful in modeling, simulation, and (sometimes) prediction. Different Python packages are available that employ machine learning and neural networks to fit given data. Training sets are used to train the modeling algorithm and then applied to fit new data to test the algorithm. For the data sets displayed, the red line represents the results from the trained model and the green line represents the actual solution returned by the function. The training set data is generated by adding noise to the set function. The keras library was used in Python for developing and evaluating these models.

I spent Spring 2020 looking into the relation of dissociation rates to the centrifugal barrier. Hypersonic re-entry flight experiences extreme temperatures leading to a state of chemical non-equilibrium in the flow. To analyze the state-to-state (StS) kinetics for these flows, we propose to fit the kinetics data using sparse regression techniques. The advantages of obtaining an expression includes providing an insight into the relation of dissociation via various levels to the centrifugal barrier and arriving at a physics-informed grouping strategy to reduce the dimension of the kinetics system. This would further aid in modeling the reactions that take place in the flow in a computationally efficient way.

Fitting the kinetics data required the use of the MATLAB curve-fitting toolbox. Messing with the different parameters yielded a variety of different initial results, with more accurate results coming from using sparse regression techniques.

Looking at the correlation between the dissociation rates and the rotational and vibrational quantum numbers also was able to be visually represented using contour plots with an accurate line of best fit:

Future research will be devoted towards analyzing the relations between other parameters as different variables impact the dissociation rates. This research shows that these rates are primarily governed by rotation, which previous studies in this field commonly neglect for simplicity. Once we can understand the extent that rotation affects the dissociation rates, these models can be used for various applications in computational fluid dynamics.
